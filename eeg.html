Building a Low Cost EEG

Introduction to Sensors, Instrumentation, and Measurements Course
Project Summary:
  • Circuits
  • Electronic Filters

The goal of this project was to design and build an electroencephalogram (EEG) that records brainwaves non-invasively through the scalp. Specifically, we were interested in distinguishing between relaxed and focused states of the user, which are frequently found to characterize various control modes in brain-computer interfaces. Our functional, relatively inexpensive EEG is a step towards a future of lower-cost, flexible solutions for the many people who cannot otherwise access such technologies.

Project Details:

A major challenge of recording neuroelectrical activity is decoding a noisy, small magnitude signal via filtering and amplification. We constructed a circuit with notch filtering to remove the surrounding powerline frequency (60 Hz) and both high and low pass filters to accentuate alpha and beta wave ranges (8-32 Hz). These have been found to correspond to relaxed and focused mental states, respectively.

EEG circuit schematic. This circuit was used in conjunction with an Analog Discovery to record and visualize the signal.

Three electrodes were placed on the user via conductive paste: a grounding electrode on the earlobe, a reference on the mastoid, and a primary signal electrode on the occipitoparietal region of the scalp.

In preliminary testing, the user was instructed to relax with their eyes closed, blink rapidly, clench their jaw, and focus while performing mental math.

A 10 Hertz wave was observable via WaveForms in real-time during relaxed states. There was also a visible difference between relaxed states and muscle movements. This visual clarity gave a preliminary confidence in the data.

The recorded trials that followed consisted of six thirty-second blocks where the user was instructed to either relax with their eyes closed or perform a mental math task with their eyes opened.

A Fast Fourier Transform was applied to the recorded trials in MATLAB, translating a time series response into the frequency domain. This allowed for a clearer analysis of alpha wave ranges.
